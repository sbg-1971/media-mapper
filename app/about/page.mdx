# About the Media Mapper

## What is it?

The Media Mapper is an open source framework intended to be used as a starting point for building a web application geared towards exploring Media throughout time and space.

The project was dreamt up by **Ennuri Jo** as a way to explore the relationship between media and water. Funding for this project was provided by the [University of Pennsylvania, Penn](https://www.upenn.edu/). The framework was developed by Ben Tyler and [Lost Creek Designs](https://lostcreekdesigns.co/).

Please see the [GitHub repository](https://github.com/Aqueous-Earth-Catalog-Team/media-mapper) for more information about how you can get started with the project!

# About Entangled Refugee Mobilities 

Entangled Refugee Mobilities retraces the trajectories of the ethnic Vietnamese from Cambodia as they were “repatriated” to South Vietnam in 1970, in the context of the Second Indochina War. 

## Historical background

In the late 1960s, anti-Vietnamese resentment ran high in the Cambodian population, as an estimated 40,000 Vietnamese communists were believed to be within Cambodia. It peaked in March-April 1970 following the coup that removed head of state Norodom Sihanouk from power. As Cambodia became more involved in the Vietnam War, the Vietnamese minority faced arrests, detentions, and violent attacks. The intervention of South Vietnamese and US troops (May-June 1970) in support to the new regime pushed the Communists deeper into Cambodia.  The spreading of bombardments and combats, the fear of being recruited by the ‘Vietcong’, and heightened tensions with Cambodians drove even more Vietnamese to flee their homes. 

Under public pressure, the Saigon government agreed to ‘repatriate’ its ‘nationals’ (some were born in Cambodia and had never set foot in Vietnam). The operation was organized jointly by a special delegation from South Vietnam and the Cambodian government. It was conducted mostly via fluvial convoys in May-October 1970. Vietnamese civilians awaited their departures in camps run either by religious denominations or Cambodian civilian authorities, with the help of international voluntary agencies. The ‘repatriation’ brought between 200,000 and 250,000 people to South Vietnam – possibly up to 300,000 if one includes the people from the border zone who left by foot or with South Vietnamese military trucks. 

## Why "entangled"? 

These mobilities were ‘entangled’ because the itineraries, means of transport, and logistics of the ‘repatriation’ overlapped with the displacements and operations of the South Vietnamese Army (ARVN) and the US Army. The Vietnamese civilians were ‘evacuated’ on military ships from the South Vietnamese Navy with American backing. These ships traveled via the Mekong River for military operations to unload troops and arms for joint operations against the Vietnamese communists inside Cambodia, and on the way back they brought the refugees. The latter often arrived in military bases in South Vietnam, and from there they were transferred to various locations through the Civil Operations and Rural Development Support (CORDS) pacification program. It is typical of what Asian American Studies scholar Yen Le Espiritu calls ‘militarized refuge(e)’ [Body Counts, University of California Press, 2014].

## What will it do?  

"Entangled Refugee Mobilities" will help understand how this type of refugeehood deployed. While the ‘repatriation’ is not an unknown case, it is still under-studied. The picture (numbers, locations, actors, processes, conditions, and timelines) remains impressionistic. To this day, there has been no oral history project. The operation was not ‘invisible’ as journalists, humanitarian organizations, governmental officials, and military photography units documented it. Yet, so far, there has not been any critical discussion of these sources. 

It will retrace these intertwined trajectories of refugees, troops, and weapons, with as many historiographical details as possible.  One of the biggest challenges is to recover the exact locations of the camps and bases and to “re-materialize” them through images, testimonies, and reports. The Media Mapper is a significant first step in this direction. What is shows is not only the outcome of the research. It is a part of the research process itself involving trial and error. As such, it is a work-in-progress and hopefully it may become a participatory tool for collective efforts to reconstruct from multiple perspectives this long-neglected history.
